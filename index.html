Before You Buy

Co-op vs. condo — what actually matters

Two very different ownership structures. One very different approval process.

Co-op
—Own shares in a corporation
—Proprietary lease for your unit
—Board approval required to buy
—Lower price point (10–20% discount)
—Higher monthly maintenance
—Sublet restrictions common
—Stricter financing rules
Condo
—Own real property outright
—No board approval to buy
—Higher purchase price
—Lower monthly common charges
—Easier to rent out
—Easier to finance and sell
—Right of first refusal only

Common Questions

What buyers and investors ask most

Straight answers to the questions that come up in every buyer consultation.

From contract signing to board decision, expect 4–8 weeks. Assembling your package takes 2–4 weeks; board review another 2–4. Under a new NYC law effective July 28, 2026, boards must acknowledge your application within 15 days and issue a decision within 45 days — or face civil penalties.
Three things: your debt-to-income ratio (most boards want combined housing costs under 28–33% of gross income), post-closing liquidity (typically 6–24 months of maintenance and mortgage in reserves), and down payment (most Manhattan co-ops require 20–25%; stricter prewar buildings sometimes demand 50% or all-cash).
Yes — co-op boards can reject applicants without stating a reason, as long as they don't violate anti-discrimination laws. This is one of the key differences between a co-op and a condo purchase. An experienced agent can help you read a building's approval culture before you make an offer.
NYC Intro. 1120-B requires boards of co-ops with 10+ units to acknowledge applications within 15 days and issue a final decision within 45 days. If they miss the 15-day window, your application is automatically deemed complete. One 14-day extension is permitted. Violations carry penalties starting at $1,000 per incident.
Co-ops historically trade at a 10–20% discount to comparable condos due to the board approval process and financing restrictions. For buyers who can clear the financial bar, that discount represents real value — especially in buildings with low underlying mortgages and strong reserve funds. Investors should confirm sublet policies before purchasing, as many co-ops restrict rentals.
When you buy a condo, you own real property. When you buy a co-op, you own shares in a corporation that owns the building, and you hold a proprietary lease for your unit. Co-ops have stricter board control, lower price points, and higher monthly maintenance (which includes a share of the building's mortgage and real estate taxes). Condos are easier to finance, sublet, and sell.
